In 1977, NASA undertook one of the most ambitious space missions in history, launching Voyager 1 and Voyager 2, two spacecraft designed to unlock the mysteries of the outer planets in our solar system.

2

For the first time, humanity saw the towering volcanic eruptions on Jupiter’s moon Io, confirming it as the most volcanically active body in the solar system.

3

Saturn’s rings, once thought to be simple, were revealed to be intricately structured, composed of countless particles ranging in size from tiny grains to massive boulders.

Voyager 1, the more distant of the two, is currently over 24 billion kilometers (approximately 15 billion miles) from Earth. It travels at a speed of about 61,000 kilometers per hour (38,000 miles per hour) relative to the Sun.

Voyager 1 is far beyond the heliosphere, the bubble-like region of space dominated by the Sun’s magnetic field and solar wind, having crossed the heliopause—the boundary of interstellar space—in August 2012.

Since then, it has been providing scientists with data about the interstellar medium, including measurements of cosmic rays, magnetic fields, and particle densities, which offer a glimpse into the environment of deep space.

However, due to its great distance, it takes more than 22 hours for a signal to travel from Voyager 1 to Earth at the speed of light.

4

Voyager 2, the only spacecraft to ever visit Uranus and Neptune, unveiled the icy landscapes and extreme weather of these far-off planets, including Neptune’s supersonic winds and massive storms.

5

These discoveries not only transformed our understanding of the outer solar system but also inspired generations of scientists and engineers.

After completing their grand tour of the planets, the spacecraft continued their mission, venturing into the uncharted territory at the edge of the solar system.

It crossed the heliopause in November 2018, entering interstellar space and becoming the second spacecraft to do so.

This data gave scientists invaluable insights into the boundary region and how the Sun’s influence interacts with the interstellar medium.

Unlike Voyager 1, Voyager 2’s plasma instruments were still operational during its crossing, allowing it to provide direct measurements of the transition from the heliosphere to interstellar space.

Voyager 2 continues to collect and transmit data about cosmic rays, magnetic fields, and plasma waves. Signals from Voyager 2 take more than 18 hours to reach Earth.

The Golden records

Both Voyager spacecraft carry a remarkable message for the cosmos: the Golden Record. This 12-inch gold-plated copper disc is a time capsule intended to communicate the essence of Earth to any intelligent extraterrestrial life that might one day encounter it.

Conceived by a team led by renowned astronomer Carl Sagan, the record encapsulates the sights, sounds, and emotions of our planet.

Though the odds of the Golden Records being discovered by an extraterrestrial civilization are extraordinarily small, their purpose transcends practicality. They symbolize humanity’s boundless curiosity, our yearning to explore, and our desire to be known in the infinite expanse of space.

As the Voyagers drift farther and farther into the cosmic abyss, billions of kilometers from home, they serve as enduring ambassadors of Earth a quiet reminder of a species that dared to dream beyond its planet.

Accompanying these auditory treasures are 116 images depicting life on Earth: human anatomy, mathematical concepts, landscapes, and everyday scenes like a mother nursing her baby.

Messages of goodwill from global leaders, including U.S. President Jimmy Carter and U.N. Secretary-General Kurt Waldheim, reflect humanity’s hope for connection beyond our solar system.
